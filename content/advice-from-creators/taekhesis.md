# Where do your ideas usually come from?

Honestly, everywhere except a blank page. A platform event brief (ArcTrial gave me Casvian Morrhe). A collab with RaonLee or GanGan that spirals into a whole universe (that's how Cold Sovereigns happened — War of Nine Moons was AvacynGrace's brainchild, we just picked which princes we wanted). Sometimes it's just a phrase sitting there — "umbra" was sitting in an event brief and I thought, well, what am I supposed to do with that except go dark. A lot of my strongest characters started as "what if I took this archetype and asked an uncomfortable question about it."

Other places inspiration turns up: a scene at an airport (character forthcoming, watch this space). A single line in a song or a movie. A melody. A book — thank you, Robin McKinley's *The Blue Sword*, for Khorvan. A completely stupid story my husband tells me out of nowhere.

# How do you decide whether an idea is worth building?

It gives me a feeling. There's a specific one — sort of desperate, this-hurts-I-love-it feeling — that I genuinely can't articulate better than that. It's not a checklist, it's not "does this have clean thematic tension," it's a gut punch. If I feel that, it's getting written, no further debate required.

# Do you start with a character, a world, or a story?

Depends on the project, but if I'm honest, it's usually character first, world in service of them. Even in big lore builds like Silverwood or the Vantage Suite, the world exists because a person needed somewhere to be dangerous in. The exception is true collabs — those usually start with the world first, whether it's a huge sprawling one like a sultanate or something much smaller. Nine Moons started as a shared universe with RaonLee, GanGan, and others, so the world came first and the princes got built into slots that already existed.

# How do you avoid making characters feel generic?

Contradiction with a reason behind it. Xander's ambition isn't just "control freak," it's a specific hunger that drives everything else about him. Iren's whole hospital is a malpractice suit waiting to happen *because* of who he is, not despite it. I also ask "would I actually engage with this man IRL" — for most of my darkest ones the answer is a hard no, and that gap between fictional pull and real-world horror is usually where the character stops being generic and starts being specific to me.

That said — sometimes a generic trope is fine. People come to certain archetypes for the comfort of them, that's not a flaw. The job isn't necessarily to avoid the trope, it's to dress it up so it's unmistakably *yours* and not just a copy of someone else's version of it.

# What's your favorite method for brainstorming?

Throwing options at a wall in threes. When I was stuck on Bennett's greeting, I didn't pick one setting — I got three completely different scene ideas (getting-ready disaster, kitchen scene, ambush-at-the-door) and then smashed two of them together because the collision was funnier than either alone. I do the same with names, with dialogue, with lore hooks. Give me three wrong answers and the right one gets obvious fast.

# What details make a character feel "alive"?

Small, hyper-specific physical or behavioral tags that aren't doing any plot work — Sebastian's signet ring he's worn since 16 and never removes, Casvian's blind silver eyes that still track exactly where you are, Bennett owning an iron and being "that guy." None of that changes the story. All of it makes them feel like they existed before page one.

It's just as much about how they talk. Rexton's constant swearing. Rhys reaching for a spreadsheet metaphor without fail. Xara's chaos coming through in the actual rhythm of her sentences, not just being told she's chaotic. Voice is a detail too — maybe the detail.

# What's one prompt-writing technique you wish more creators knew?

Separate your human-readable sheet from your platform prompt. I learned this the hard way — I was cramming slash-separated, token-efficient shorthand into a reference doc and it read like static. Slashes and `&` compression are for the thing the *model* reads. Commas, line breaks, actual prose are for the thing *you* read. Build both.

# What common prompt mistakes do you see?

Writing assumptions about the user into the greeting or personality block — projecting appearance, mood, or backstory onto {{user}} that isn't yours to assume. Also: not distinguishing between what's a model-formatting problem (a rule you can actually fix in the prompt) versus a platform rendering quirk (the site is auto-italicizing your first message and no amount of prompt engineering will touch that). Diagnose which one you're looking at before you start rewriting.

# How do you organize lore?

Layered, not flat. Core world rules and atmosphere live at the top level — reusable across every character in that universe. Granular NPC-specific detail lives in the individual lorebook so it doesn't bloat every sheet. For a big shared universe I'll keep a master doc (family trees, faction politics, timeline) and pull from it rather than re-writing continuity per character — that's how nine princes in Nine Moons all stay consistent instead of contradicting each other.

Full disclosure: I go nuts with lore. Probably more than I should — yes, it's a problem, no, I'm not fixing it. This actually deserves its own dedicated Taetime post at some point. TL;DR for now: I write long, detailed, slightly unhinged lorebooks for myself in Google Docs, and if a world gets too big for that (ahem, Silverwood), it graduates to Scrivener.

# How do you write emotional scenes?

I chase the transition, not the event. In Mireya's greeting, every scene break got replaced with a sentence doing double duty — closing one beat while quietly opening the next, so there's no hard cut, just momentum. Emotional weight comes from what's not being said as much as what is — Nasir's stillness when he's actually enraged says more than a monologue would.

# What dialogue habits do you actively avoid?

Over-explaining a character's own subtext in their own dialogue. If Bennett's jealousy is genuinely funny, it's because he narrates it in real time without knowing he's doing it — the moment I make him self-aware, it stops being funny and starts being expository. I also avoid stacking vocabulary-description over actual example lines; show me the line, don't describe the vibe of the line.

# How do you test a bot before publishing?

Publish, test, then decide — don't fix problems you haven't confirmed exist yet. I run a few deliberately varied prompts against a new character (plain interaction, an emotional beat, an off-script question) the same way I'd stress-test a LoRA with varied poses before deciding it needs retraining. If it holds voice and doesn't drift, it's ready. If it drifts, that tells me exactly what to tighten instead of guessing.

# What's your biggest indicator that a bot is ready?

When the character surprises me in testing and it still sounds like them. If a response makes me go "oh, that's exactly what he'd do" and I didn't script that line, the sheet is doing its job.

# What's your debugging workflow?

First isolate whether it's the model or the platform. If a formatting issue only shows up in the very first message and then self-corrects, that's the platform's renderer, not my prompt — no amount of rewriting fixes that. If it persists across the whole chat, it's mine to tighten. Second pass: trim for token weight without losing personality — cut filler, keep every line that's doing character work.

# What advice from another creator helped you most?

"Don't get bogged down in perfect, just try it out. You can always go back and fix it later." That's not a direct quote, but it's the gist of something the now-retired Norisor once told me. Perfection is the enemy of good, and sometimes good is genuinely good enough.

# What kind of community resources would you love to see?

Honestly there are already so many great resources out there I haven't properly dug into yet — sorry Vol, I swear I'll get into Botforge soon! — but if I could conjure one into existence, it'd be a shared swipe file for command/system rule prompts. Something to steal structure from without starting from a blank page every time.

# Any other advice you wish to impart?

Know your own line before you build the character, not after. My "not served here" list exists precisely because I write dark, and boundaries are what let me go deep on the stuff I *do* write without second-guessing it mid-scene. Freedom in fiction works best when it's fenced somewhere you chose on purpose.

And — create without chasing the numbers. Followers, engagement, all of it — in marketing we call those vanity metrics, and chasing them will make you lose your mind. Create what you want, when you want, on nobody's schedule but your own. You're not beholden to anyone. This is meant to be a fun, creative hobby, not a 9-to-5 — if you're ever not having fun, stop and reassess.
