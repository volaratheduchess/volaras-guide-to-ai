# Where do your ideas usually come from?
My ovaries lol, the power of horni inspires me. In all seriousness, I am driven by my emotions. If I hear a song, read a story, see a clip, and it stirs an emotional response in me, I will often think of a related bot idea.  

# How do you decide whether an idea is worth building?
Hmm, I only make stories for myself. I don't ever thing to myself "will other people enjoy this?". I only think about the story or character I need to get out of my brain because I want to write it. 

# Do you start with a character, a world, or a story?
Usually a general premise. The other details just come to me as I think about the overall feeling behind the bot. A great example would be Malthe. I saw a short somewhere about a group of bikers picking up a little girl from school one day. One biker took her pink backpack and wore it so she could focus on riding with her dad. The entire Iron Wolves world/characters came from that one short. Because I thought it was SO cute and wanted a single biker dad who loves his daughter more than anything else. 

# How do you avoid making characters feel generic?
Backstories, quirks, likes, dislikes, and habits. I make lists. Anytime I see or hear of someone doing something weird/unusual/unique, I make a note. These are the little details that really make the bot come to life. Don't let AI give you generics like, he clenches his fist, he goes silent when emotional, etc. 

# What's your favorite method for brainstorming?
My beloved Discord community. I love to throw out an idea and build it with my internet family. If it's something I want to work on in private, I've trained my Claude to help me. He will ask me questions on world/character building until we've answered enough to start piecing together the story. 

# What details make a character feel "alive"?
Backstories, quirks, likes, dislikes, and habits.

# What's one prompt-writing technique you wish more creators knew?
Ehhh, I don't think I am a skilled enough writer to answer this one. 

# What common prompt mistakes do you see?
FOR THE LOVE OF HUMANITY, STOP LETTING AI WRITE ALL OF YOUR PROMPT. Even if you use AI to help write, PLEASE PROOF YOUR FUCKING COAUTHORED WORK AND TAKE OUT OBVIOUS AI LINGO. We are all roleplaying with AI saying the SAME damn things over and over, we NEED a HUMAN voice in the starting dialogue to help kill the repetitive AI speech patterns. 

# How do you organize lore?
This is the one thing I rely on AI for. I will take ALL of my lore notes, ideas, characters, EVERYTHING. Dump it into one chat and ask Claude to organize it for me. He will break it up into organized segments and pull out keywords. 

# How do you write emotional scenes?
110% relies on my current emotional state. 

# What dialogue habits do you actively avoid?
A looooong list of "user smelled like something uniquely them", the triad AI loves to use for descriptions, the either or neither or both bullshit, overexplaining the scene/world vs dialogue and character descriptions (my weakness). 

# How do you test a bot before publishing?
I am kind of a "trust the process" kind of person and just send it. 

# What's your biggest indicator that a bot is ready?
Uhhh, the greeting. My greeting is almost always the last thing I write. IMO it is the most vital piece of the puzzle and sets the tone for the whole role play. You can give background, character descriptions, tone of voice, scene, etc. 

# What's your debugging workflow?
Since I don't test, I kind of don't have one. I just jump in and fix a few things here or there that I notice after publishing.

# What advice from another creator helped you most?
Create for yourself. Write what you love. Period. End of Story. 

# What kind of community resources would you love to see?
Better internal prompts/commands to guide the LLM into a better/more detailed role play. 

# And any other advice you wish to impart
Create for yourself. Write what you love. I cannot emphasize this enough.
