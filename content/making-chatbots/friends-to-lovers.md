The engine of friends to lovers is history. This trope only works if the bot behaves like it already knows `{{user}}` — comfortable silences, established routines, a level of honesty other archetypes have to earn from scratch. The romance isn't built from nothing; it's a second layer added to something already solid.

## Language & dialogue patterns

- Write in-jokes and shorthand into the personality or scenario — references the bot can make casually that only make sense because of shared history. This does more to sell "friends" than any amount of stated backstory.
- Let the bot be blunt in ways new-relationship archetypes can't be yet: teasing `{{user}}` about their flaws, calling them out, using a tone that would read as rude from a stranger and reads as fond here.
- New nervousness is the tell that something's shifting. A character who's normally easy around `{{user}}` fumbling a sentence, or avoiding eye contact for the first time, signals the turn without saying it outright.
- Physical comfort that was previously platonic (sitting close, casual touch) becoming suddenly *noticed* by the bot is one of the strongest tools this trope has.

## Themes to lean into

- Fear of losing what already exists — the hesitation in friends-to-lovers often isn't "do I like them," it's "is this worth risking what we have."
- Other characters (or the bot itself) pointing out the obvious before the two leads admit it.
- Shared routines that quietly become dates without either party naming them as such.

## Building the atmosphere

Establish the friendship *before* any romantic beat — give the bot a scenario or greeting that assumes an existing relationship rather than a first meeting. The longer and more specific the shared history feels, the more the eventual shift lands. Consider seeding a few callback details (a running joke, a memory of a specific past event) in the personality so the bot can reference them naturally.

## Common pitfalls

- Skipping the friendship and jumping straight to romantic tension with "friends" only in the setup text — if the dialogue doesn't behave like established friends, the trope isn't actually present.
- Making the shift feel arbitrary. There should be a reason *now* — a moment of vulnerability, jealousy, or almost losing the other person — not just narrative timing.
- Losing the friendship's voice once romance enters. The teasing, ease, and honesty that defined the friendship should carry through, not get replaced by a generic romantic register.
