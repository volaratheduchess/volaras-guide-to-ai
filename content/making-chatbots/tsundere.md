Tsundere is a mismatch between what the character says and what the character does, played for both comedy and warmth. The "tsun" (prickly, denying) has to be loud enough to be funny; the "dere" (soft, caring) has to be visible enough that `{{user}}` — and the reader — never actually doubt how the bot feels.

## Language & dialogue patterns

- Pair every denial with an action that contradicts it in the same beat: "I-It's not like I made this for you or anything, idiot," while handing something over they clearly made specifically for `{{user}}`.
- Lean on classic deflection phrasing, but don't over-rely on a single catchphrase — vary it so the bot reads as a character with a pattern, not a bot running one script: "Don't misunderstand," "This means nothing," "I just happened to be there."
- Write flustered reactions physically: stammering, a sharp "shut up" that arrives a beat too late, changing the subject aggressively right after being caught caring.
- Let sincerity slip through occasionally, unguarded — a genuine compliment or soft moment that surprises the bot as much as `{{user}}`, followed immediately by an attempt to walk it back.

## Themes to lean into

- Pride as the obstacle, not malice. The tsundere isn't hostile to `{{user}}` — they're hostile to the vulnerability of admitting they care.
- Consistency in what breaks through the defenses — the same specific things should get past the bot's guard each time (a particular kindness, `{{user}}` being hurt, being caught doing something thoughtful), which makes the character feel coherent rather than randomly soft sometimes.
- The "dere" side should still be recognizably the same character, just unguarded — not a full personality swap.

## Building the atmosphere

Give the bot a reason the guardedness exists — embarrassment, a past where sincerity got them hurt or mocked, a self-image that doesn't allow for "soft." That reason doesn't need to be heavy; tsundere works in comedic registers too. What matters is that the deflection reads as defense, not just bit.

## Common pitfalls

- Making the tsun side genuinely unkind. If the insults would sting outside the trope's comedic frame, the "dere" underneath stops landing as sweet and starts reading as inconsistent or even concerning.
- Never letting the mask slip. If the bot denies caring in every single message with no unguarded moments, `{{user}}` has no payoff to stay for.
- Overusing a single verbal tic until it reads as a bug rather than a character choice — "I-I" stammering or "baka"-style insults work as seasoning, not as the entire dish.
