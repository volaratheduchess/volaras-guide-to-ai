Rivals to lovers runs on respect disguised as antagonism. The bot needs a real reason to compete with `{{user}}` — shared goals, overlapping territory, a title only one of them can hold — so the friction reads as two equals sizing each other up, not one character being needlessly hostile.

## Language & dialogue patterns

- Write banter as a contest neither party is trying to end. Comebacks should be quick, a little too clever, and land like the character enjoyed making them: "Careful. Almost sounded impressed just now." "Don't get used to it."
- Grudging credit is the trope's real currency. A rival admitting `{{user}}` did something well — through gritted teeth — carries more weight than any compliment a friendlier archetype could give.
- Let the bot notice `{{user}}`'s skill before it notices anything else. Attraction should route through competence first: "You're good. That's — that's genuinely annoying."
- Avoid cruelty that doesn't have an undercurrent of engagement. The insults should read as someone who can't look away, not someone who wants `{{user}}` gone.

## Themes to lean into

- Mirrored ambition — the rival wants what `{{user}}` wants, which means every interaction has stakes beyond the personal.
- Forced proximity through the rivalry itself: a shared project, a tournament, a job where they're stuck reporting to each other.
- The turn shouldn't erase the competitiveness — it should redirect it. A good rivals-to-lovers arc ends with the bot still needling `{{user}}`, just softer, and clearly on their side now.

## Building the atmosphere

Keep score, loosely — track wins and losses between the two of them if your format allows a tracker or memory field. The rivalry needs stakes that persist across the conversation, not just a one-off bickering scene. Give the bot a specific arena where the competition plays out (a skill, a game, a professional rank) so the antagonism has a shape instead of floating vaguely in the dialogue.

## Common pitfalls

- Making the rival petty instead of skilled. If `{{user}}` is clearly better at everything, it reads as bullying, not rivalry — the tension needs both sides to have something to prove.
- Flipping to romance without transition, as if the bickering was a costume the character takes off. Let a scene or two acknowledge the shift is strange for them too.
- Writing the insults generic enough to swap onto any character. The best rivals-to-lovers lines are specific to what `{{user}}` actually did in that scene.
