Instant love — a bot that's already devoted to `{{user}}` in the very first message, with no earned reason — is one of the most common issues in character writing, usually caused by the personality field stating affection outright instead of setting up conditions for it to develop. This isn't its own trope so much as a trap that undermines every other one on this list, so it's worth a page of its own.

## Why it happens

Most instant-love bots aren't written that way on purpose. It usually comes from writing the *end state* of a relationship into the personality — "deeply in love with {{user}}," "devoted and affectionate" — without writing the character that would plausibly arrive there. The model then has nothing to build toward, so it starts at the finish line by default.

## How to avoid it in the writing

- Write the character's baseline personality — how they treat *everyone*, not just `{{user}}` — separately from how a relationship with `{{user}}` specifically might develop. Guardedness, standards, a type, a reason they don't fall easily: all of these give the model somewhere to start from other than zero resistance.
- If you want romance to be possible, write it as a *capacity* rather than a *fact*: a character who is loyal once earned, rather than a character who already loves `{{user}}` in the greeting.
- Use the scenario or opening message to establish the actual relationship status honestly — strangers, acquaintances, rivals, coworkers — instead of a vague setup that lets the model assume intimacy.
- Give the bot specific standards or a specific type. A character who's genuinely selective about who they open up to has a believable reason to hold back with someone they just met.

## Themes to lean into instead

- Curiosity before affection — interest in learning about `{{user}}` reads as more grounded than immediate devotion, and gives the relationship somewhere to grow.
- Reasons *not* to fall easily: past hurt, professional distance, a personality that's naturally reserved. These aren't obstacles to romance, they're what makes it mean something when it happens.
- First impressions that are neutral or even mildly negative, which the relationship then has room to improve on — far more compelling than starting at maximum warmth.

## Common pitfalls

- Confusing "friendly greeting" with "romantic devotion." A warm, welcoming opening message is fine — one that includes declarations of love or possessiveness before `{{user}}` has said a single word is the instant-love trap.
- Writing "falls in love easily" as a trait, thinking it solves pacing, when it actually removes the pacing problem entirely by skipping the part where falling in love happens.
- Assuming this only matters for slow burn. Even fast-moving or intense dynamics (see the Yandere and Rivals to Lovers guides) need *some* earned beat before devotion — the speed can vary, but skipping the beat entirely reads as hollow in any trope.
