Reading other people's bots is one of the fastest ways to improve, but there's a real difference between studying *how* something works and copying *what* it says. This page is about the first one — picking apart technique the same way you'd study any craft, without lifting someone else's original writing.

## What to actually look at

Don't just read a bot's personality and ask "do I like this character." Ask more specific, technical questions:

- **Structure** — how is the personality field organized? What comes first, what's saved for last? Compare a few bots you admire against the ordering suggestions on the [Writing Personality](#making-chatbots-bot-creation-writing-personality) page — do they agree, or did the creator find a different order that works for their style?
- **Opening messages** — what does the first message accomplish? Does it establish scenario, voice, and a hook for `{{user}}` to respond to, all in a few lines? Greetings are one of the highest-leverage places to study, because a weak one sinks an otherwise good bot.
- **Dialogue rhythm** — short, clipped sentences versus long descriptive ones; how much internal narration versus spoken dialogue; how often the bot asks questions versus makes statements. This is usually the most distinctive fingerprint a creator has.
- **Formatting choices** — italics for actions, asterisks, quotation marks, or none of the above. Notice what a creator uses consistently, and notice when they break their own pattern for effect.
- **What's left out** — sometimes the most instructive thing about a well-written bot is what it *doesn't* over-explain. Restraint is a skill, and it's easy to miss if you're only looking at what's on the page.

## How to take notes without taking words

A useful habit: after reading a bot you admire, close the tab and write down *why* it worked in your own words before doing anything else. If you can't rephrase the technique without your notes turning into a close paraphrase of their actual text, you've understood the surface, not the mechanism — go back and look again for the underlying choice (pacing, structure, restraint) rather than the specific phrasing.

It helps to separate two different things you might be reacting to:

- **Technique** — reusable: pacing, structure, how tension is built, how a personality field is organized. Completely fair game to learn from and apply to your own original characters.
- **Voice and content** — not reusable: specific lines, a character's exact backstory, distinctive turns of phrase that are part of what makes that creator's work theirs. Admire it, don't lift it.

## Where to look

- Highly-favorited or trending bots on whichever site you use are an easy starting point, precisely because popularity is (imperfect but real) signal that something in the writing is working.
- Creators who post breakdowns or guides of their own process are especially valuable to study, since they're explaining their reasoning rather than leaving you to reverse-engineer it.
- Your own favorite bots to *chat with*, not just read — behavior in an actual conversation reveals things a static personality field can't, like how the bot handles a topic change or an unexpected question.

## A note on credit

If a specific creator's approach genuinely shaped how you write — a structural idea, a formatting convention, a technique you picked up from watching them work — it costs nothing to say so publicly. Crediting the people you learned from is good practice in this community, not a legal formality.
